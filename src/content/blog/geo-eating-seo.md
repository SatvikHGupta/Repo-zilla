# Adobe Just Paid $1.9B to Admit SEO Is Dying. Here's What Replaces It.

In November 2025, Adobe agreed to buy Semrush, a company best known for keyword research and SEO tooling, for roughly $1.9 billion in an all-cash deal. Adobe's own announcement frames Semrush as a brand-visibility platform whose data-driven generative engine optimization (GEO) and search engine optimization capabilities help marketers manage visibility and audience reach. That's a company built for the SEO era being bought specifically for the thing that comes after it.

## What "GEO" actually is, and where the term came from

This isn't a marketing buzzword someone made up last quarter. The term "Generative Engine Optimization" was formally introduced in a November 2023 paper by researchers from Princeton University, Georgia Tech, the Allen Institute for AI, and IIT Delhi. The paper defines it as a framework to help content creators improve their visibility inside generative-engine responses through a black-box optimization approach, since creators otherwise have little control over when and how their content gets surfaced by tools like ChatGPT or Bing Chat.

The researchers didn't just coin a phrase. They measured it. Their benchmark, GEO-bench, covered 10,000 queries across nine domains, and the tested techniques showed they could lift a source's visibility in AI-generated answers by up to 40%. The tactics that worked best weren't exotic: naming credentialed experts, citing sources inline, and adding concrete statistics. Keyword-stuffing an article for a search engine crawler does basically nothing for a language model deciding what to cite.

## Why Adobe is willing to pay for this specifically

Semrush pulled in $376.8 million in revenue last year, up 22% year-over-year, and Adobe's own analytics showed traffic from generative-AI sources to U.S. retail sites jumped 1,200% year-over-year in October. That kind of growth curve is what justifies a nine-figure acquisition for a company whose core product (SEO tooling) is arguably becoming less relevant on its own. Adobe isn't buying the old business. It's buying the pivot.

## The part that should actually change how you write

The most citable finding making the rounds right now isn't from the Princeton paper. It's from separate large-scale analysis of real ChatGPT citation behavior. One analysis of roughly 3 million ChatGPT responses and 30 million citations found that 44.2% of all citations come from the first 30% of a page's content, dropping off sharply after that. The researcher behind it calls this a "ski ramp" pattern: a steep drop after the opening third, then a long, thin tail for everything after.

Why would that be true? These models are trained heavily on journalism and academic writing that front-load conclusions, so they seem to weight early framing more heavily and read the rest of the piece through that initial lens.

So the classic SEO-era structure, slow buildup, keyword-rich middle, conclusion at the end, ends up being close to the worst possible shape for a piece you want an LLM to cite. If your actual answer is buried at paragraph twelve, you're optimizing for a reader pattern that answer engines don't follow anymore.

## The honest caveat

Before you rewrite everything: the 44.2% figure comes from one large independent analysis (later republished and re-cited across a lot of marketing blogs), not from the original Princeton/Georgia Tech paper itself. A few secondary sources conflate the two. The underlying finding is consistent and well-corroborated across multiple write-ups, but it's a correlational pattern in observed citation behavior, not a controlled experiment like GEO-bench was. Treat "front-load your answer" as strongly supported advice, not an immutable law of how every model works.

## SEO Isn't Dead, the Last Mile Changed

SEO isn't dead. A widely cited industry estimate puts roughly 80% of good GEO practice as just fundamentals SEO already required, but the last mile has changed. Ranking on a results page and getting cited inside a synthesized answer are now two different games, and the second one rewards clarity and front-loaded substance over the old game's patience.
